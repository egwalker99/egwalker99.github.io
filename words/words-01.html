talk show

I'm pleased to have with us on the program today one of the primary curators of the Third Planet Natural History Museum and Sanctuary. Welcome, and thank you for being with us today.


Of course. It’s my pleasure.


Now, this restoration project was an extraordinary undertaking.


Yes, the cleanup alone was a monumental task, of not only ridding the planet of the parasitic element, but also of course correcting various biological and chemical imbalances that resulted in the poor health of the orb itself.


The parasite was in fact in danger of defeating the orb’s immune responses.


That’s correct. By the time the project began, many of the antibodies normally employed had been rendered useless, and the planet was riddled with wounds, ulcerations, and infectious material, numerous circulatory and respiratory diseases, as well as a thin cloud of parasitic metabolites surrounding its outermost layers, even beyond its immediate atmosphere. But we’ve been able to eliminate all that successfully and now the orb is surrounded by an appropriate forcefield, designed to prevent any reintroduction by any human parasites that may have escaped.


And that is monitored and updated regularly.


Oh most certainly.


One question on our listeners’ minds, I’m sure, has to do with the remaining small colonies of parasite that were left to thrive. Wouldn't the elimination entirely of the parasitic population be beneficial?


Yes, well, that was very much a consideration thoughout the planning phases of the restoration, although we were able to determine the presence of small sub-groups persisting without the self-destructive genetic defect and which were, in fact, instrumental in the cleanup phases.  Without them, the resources we would have had to expend would have been astronomical, so to speak, and the process not least of all quite time-consuming.  And at this perilous stage of the cancer, time was of the essence.  It shouldn't be overlooked, either, that throughout the parasite’s history, there have regularly appeared mutant populations with astonishing creativity and insight, minus the destructive element. So as a scientific and philosophical curiosity, we are hoping to evolve and define any of these positive genetic tendencies that yet may remain.


Tell us something of the lives of these isolated cultures you have preserved.


Yes. Well, it is a sometimes treacherous balance, keeping the herds self-contained within a prescribed range yet sufficiently occupied mentally and physiologically such that they remain within that range.  The means to self-sufficiency must be present, but limited, and the urge to swarm must be repressed at all cost.  Problems had arisen in the pre-sanctuary past, as we all know well, when those defective colonies would spread to the degree that other colonies were encountered.  A mutual infection would typically take place leading inevitably to vast ecological blight.  In light of such historical patterns, we have learned to allow minimal nomadic behavior of the preserved species-type, but only in strictly limited regions, and always in isolation from other tribes.  And of course their range excludes all but the most rudimentary contact with other lifeforms.  So each culture's development remains confined to its own... geographic petri dish, if you will, and the desirable patterns are more readily influenced.


And how are these patterns maintained?


Well, we use primarily a system of deterrents.  Isolation, amputation, and sacrifice have been very effective in keeping the aspirations of the colonies in check.


Truly impressive.  And I understand there have also been genetic modifications to further ensure limitations of the species?


Indeed.  Here for example we are looking at a colony along the Nile River that has successfully reverted to its prehistory state. Their range is limited to the irrigative properties of the river –


Look how clean it is.


Yes, it’s quite an improvement over the metabolic scarring that was present in the most leprous stage of the orb’s illness. The colony is allowed to thrive at a limited capacity as long as it remains self-sustaining. Regarding your question, we have successfully altered the organisms' reproductive systems to reduce the procreative urge, which as you know was among the rampant genetic defects that led to parasitic behaviors and ultimately the species’ failure and decline; as well we have limited the capability to reproduce to once annually, with sexual maturity reached only after about two to three decades.  We have also designed copulation to be a somewhat unpleasant experience, and of course the overall life-span has been reduced to about forty years.  In this way we interrupt past dilemmas regarding scarcity of resources due to overpopulation -- further stemming the desire to venture beyond their imposed boundaries.  And by discouraging diversity of genetic pools, we are better equipped to keep undesirable behaviors in check.


There have been occasions of parasitic tendencies to arise, however.


Correct, it’s rare but it does come up. We manage it before it becomes destructive with, as I alluded to earlier, public displays of humiliation, torture, ceremonial sacrifice. Very effective deterrent when practiced swiftly and consistently. A code of conduct is built into the social structure that must be adhered to for the preservation of the colony.


Can you give us an example of  one of these deterrents?


Sure.  Here we see members of a group eradicating a problematic individual by use of a device known historically as a "catherine wheel." 


Gruesome.


Gruesome, indeed, but its function is threefold:  It removes the problem element from the group, provides transitory entertainment, while also acting as a profound element of reinforcement of the social mores. 


What are the dangers of this? Are there not organisms that develop a cleverness on their own or with another that are able to mask the aberrent tendency, surreptitiously planning revolt?


As I said, any aberration of that sort is dealt with quite swiftly. No experimentation is permitted. At the first sign of potential aberration the member is removed, maimed, or destroyed, and thus we find that fear undermines any deviance.  In the past, during the organism’s self-governing history, their weak system of ‘multiple chances’ was a primary flaw that had helped to evolve group experimentation with uprisings, slavery, great wars and the like.


What about oppression? From the upper ranks, that is.


There is no ‘hierarchy,’ per se. The elder members are keepers of the rules and rituals. It is understood that decisions for the good of the colony are made by them. This primitive approach had been a successful pre-sanctuary model in rare circumstances.


But to the contrary, in the past it has precisely been the elders who have abused the control factor.


True. But in many cases, if not all, the abuses were related to absurd notions of territorial expansion and material wealth. Having eliminated these urges, the very idea of them in fact, much of the aberration that had driven power-abuse is eliminated.


What if you were to discover evidence of these aberrations re-establishing within the colonies?


Oh, well, then we would just vaporize them. Or feed them to the tigers.

 

belly

Zretl was a pale, bluish color, with a short dull head that was almost black.  He suffered a gestational defect that caused his lower arms to be shorter than his upper arms.  He had engineered the primary teleporting synthechronometron before the Impact of the Seventh Moon, and thus many of the modules were now still safe underground.  

With his shorter arms, he had been able to polish the scales on his own belly to a kind of mirror finish – and the strange red birdlike creature he kept in his vicinity was frequently seen tapping madly at its own reflection – an activity that, while amusing, gave Zretl indigestion, not to mention innumerable pockmarks on his surface that were frequently lined with grit and saliva, marring an otherwise radiant purity.

wind power

I realize this story might be incredible, but it really is true, there were actually at one time aliens on this planet.  Visitors from another world. When I describe them to you, you will no doubt be skeptical, as i said — incredulous. But nevertheless, it is all based in fact.  I mean, it was so long ago, and we’ve retold the story many times, and one has to account for faulty memories and various embellishments; we are, no secret there, a self-absorbed race.

It absolutely is true that I was there and was instrumental in putting an end to the infestation, or ‘visitation,’ or (a laughable supposition) invasion effort. Not for the first visits, those I had only heard about.  But I saw them that last and final time.

Let me first explain their strange aspect — these beings were actually corporeal, if you can even get your mind around that. Yes, like the rocks and ground, solid, with discernible mass…. but somehow also like the Fire Lake, I have to say, in that they moved about on their own.  Isn’t that something?  Quite clumsily, I might add!  No fluidity, no grace.  Well, their stone dropped to ground like so many other stones from outside our Inhabited Layers, but it was an unusual stone in that it came apart and then, from inside it, some very little moving stones emerged, wobbling about and rearranging so many even  smaller and smaller stones.

Now, I know it’s generally accepted that corporeal entities are not capable of supporting life as we know it, beyond the imitation by such pseudo-living phenomena as the Fire Lake and the Liquid Reserves.  But, well, evidence of this experience indicates otherwise, as these motive rock-beings clearly had direction and purpose, or shall I suggest the semblance of such things.  Obviously we have nothing to compare any of this to in recent memory.

Those that witnessed the scene reported that the corporeal pseudo-life forms scratched about, trying feebly to express themselves… but it was an absurd comedy, watching them put bits of our rock into their own peculiar rock, then go about just generally littering the area with their silly alien debris. Eventually — and this was most astonishing and delightful — eventually they congealed at last into their primary stone, which soon after emitted a plasma heat similar again but in some other way to our own Fire Lake (though they could not have known of its existence, so distant from their scurrying), and then, utilizing the heat, their stone gained altitude and at last departed from our more familiar Layers!

So amusing!  We puzzled over this.  Were we to imagine these wiggly rock monsters possessed of some rudimentary intelligence?  Yes, it is extremely difficult to envision the idea of something with mass as having life, recognizable life, let alone any degree of intent, or, even more ludicrous, self-awareness.

…By the third or fourth visitation — for by now we had to admit the experience to be deliberate, and not merely anomalous meteoric phenomena — the strange smooth glossy space-rock that fell down to us had grown quite a bit larger than it had been in the earlier times, even larger than some of the small detached stones in our Valleys.  From inside of it emerged quite a number of the alien things, and over time they had actually constructed in the vicinity some very elaborate masses resembling caves and boulders, albeit on a miniaturized scale, and quite unnatural-looking.  How comical! Such grand efforts necessary to entertain themselves so pitifully, assuming that was what they were up to.  So we were to imagine.  Why else would they be here?

However, they began to perform some rather irritating rituals, getting in the way, as it were.  For at first we were being polite, avoiding them, leaving a wide berth, moving our storms and wind carnivals and electrical displays elsewhere.  In the past, they had always left after a very brief period, but in this instance they remained, and began spreading out, and in time came upon the sacred Valley and the Fire Lake.  This is about when I got wind of the gravity of the situation, pardon the puns.

I had been into town to acquire some lighter gases in order to ascend to a surveying Layer.  You see, I was getting plans underway for the Exhibition: we all thought we might like some diversion of our own sort, since the rock folk were becoming rather tiresome.  While in town I learned that one of us had gotten all into a cyclone over these beings being in the Valley and interfering with the usual spontaneity of the rock slides.  Really, how much fun is a controlled avalanche of limited expanse?  I sympathized and offered to heat up at the Fire Lake and meet later at one of the Poles — my comrade could arrive there beforehand to chill for awhile, then at the appointed moment we would collide and release some chaos into the atmosphere, let off some steam as the saying goes.  Farther out, away from this nuisance of the creeping crawling rock creatures from the outer limits.

My friend agreed, and during the course of our engaging weather outburst in the North, for which we had to travel quite far and put off other endeavors, I began to acquire a similar point of view, that the silly Invaders had to go, their welcome worn.  Why, wasn’t it just recently that I had been settled, napping, in a favorite outcropping, when a band of these pests came bumbling past with their minuscule little stormclouds encased in tiny pebbles, utterly oblivious to my presence, and waking me with bothersome little shocks.

Now, I can produce lightning like nobody’s business.  But that’s not the point.  It was just the utter rudeness of being roused from slumber by a stupid moving stone, sentient or not it was still very annoying as I had a bit of a dust-storm hangover that morning.

Anyway, to put an end to a rambling tale, and I’m sure you realize by now that this truly is based on historical events of which I was personally a part, let me just tell you that word spread quickly once we returned and confessed our aggravation, and so spread a collective disdain for the pests, who no longer earned our amusement.  So that season we held a massive Exhibition, with gloriously choreographed tornadoes, our rarefications seeping underground and expanding to burst great broiling bubbles from the Fire Lake, catapulting molten stone into the Valley to blast apart the vermins' dull little party, and blackened clouds condensed and froze to rain down cold pointed shards upon the scene.  Liquid lifted from the Reserves was summoned to the region to crash down around the infestation in one mighty breaking wave.  Their structures were hoisted and dashed to pieces against the surrounding cliffs, suspended in whorls of our powerful gases along with our own much prettier stones, and all spun round and round in a hilarious blender — and I, I was the one who brought the great icy chunk down upon their biggest, glossiest, stupidest rock, the one they arrived in.

All around the little monsters dispersed and congealed in such marvelous disarray, I swear to you there’s not been such entertainment since.

 

another day in Anuraphagia

A frog sat at the edge of the little stone-encircled pool by the entrance to the Castle.  This frog was HUGE.  Like, unnaturally so.  Like, size of a guinea pig.  Sitting motionless.  Frogs have these peculiar bulging eyeballs that don’t seem to be looking in any direction — what’s probably the case, and this speculation could be  verified with minimal research hours, is that those freaky goatlike horizontal pupils of theirs allow them to see the nigh entire horizon, in almost any direction along the panorama of their little stationary froggy vistas.

This big freak bullfrog launched suddenly into the water upon its prey — belly flopping amongst the lilypads and duckweed, catching the attention of a travelling passerby.   My stars, this person thought, what a frighteningly enormous frog, and goodness, wouldya lookit that, it’s just caught itself a meal… hm, what’s that it’s eating.  Is that a dragonfly… wait — is that —

Zoom in to the big bobbing giant frog head, and Oh, gross! What — is that …. is that FEET?  That’s… that’s confounded frog feet, that giant frog is eating another smaller frog!  Would you think?  I mean — lookit! holy — !

The other person travelling alongside was thoroughly repulsed and wandered off to look at a rickety display of t-shirts and postcards at the edge of the flat gravelly road.

But the engrossed traveller marveled at this spectacle, at the big bullfrog floating quietly, gulping on occasion which caused the little legs to quiver and disappear bit by bit in little fits.  The smaller frog inside the enormous frog must have been carrying on quite a  besmothered struggle in there because the behemoth frog reached up from beneath the duckweed with both arms and grabbed a little foot in each of its hands in an effort to steady the calamity.  Little toes twitched: the bullfrog kept its cool.   Eventually the sad and angry feet vanished down the voracious gullet.

Meanwhile, two other average-sized frogs sat huddled together in miserable terror amongst some rocks at the opposite edge of the pool, taking in Nature's gruesome matinee -- the one frog’s arm, this is totally true, draped over the back of its companion, one might imagine clutching fearfully.

Did you know there have been seen in some regions pelicans swallowing live ducks?  Shocking.  The traveller was fascinated yet horrified.  Endowed with strong feelings of empathy and a painfully vivid imagination, disturbing notions quickly accumulated as to the little frog’s terrible terrible death experience.  A horror beyond human horrors.  The sudden stupefaction of clamping darkness after being ripped so violently from an everyday ho-hum meditative moment at home under the peaceful setting sun.  The traumatic, crashing shift in consciousness.  Then the subsequent slow suffocation, the slick completeness of a peristaltic throaty grip and nothing to look forward to but churning acid stomach, or whatever the science of frog digestion.

Nightmare images rushed forth in the traveller’s mind of a world in which animals and humans lived solely to consume those smaller versions of themselves in order to grow indefinitely large.   The traveller's eyes drifted toward the distracted companion, still perusing postcards, and a distressing soliloquy spilled forth: 'I can't help imagining some humongous fellow with a huge frightening head and a mouth half a meter in diameter plowing down upon you unexpectedly and slowly sucking you into his esophagus.  Your face trapped in a giant ribcage, this monstrous human appetite lifting your torso and flailing legs to the heavens for a gravity-assist toward some gruesome stench-filled melting hideous demise.  Really, mere words cannot describe this hellish vision.  And then — and then who’s to say there wouldn’t be another even bigger than —'

The castle doors opened and the queen came delicately out across the moat to the roadway to find the two travellers lingering by the royal frog pool.

'Isn’t it lovely?  The king made it himself.  There’s an enormous frog in there, even bigger than that fat one you see beside the lily.  It’s just remarkable!  But you know', lamented the queen, no longer boasting, 'there used to be dozens of frogs in the royal frog pool, so many, he collected them.  Not as big as those.  But somehow they’ve all gone.  The king so enjoyed them when he was still with us.  Before he…. well, I can’t imagine what’s become of them, of any of them.'

The weary traveller straightened and sighed heavily.

 

road reversal

So a squirrel was driving down the road one evening, it was dark, i don’t know how dark i wasn’t there, i only heard about it.  It had been raining earlier, that i remember, because i remember the day, i was polishing my antlers for the festival.  Squirrel shifted in his seat.  It’d been a long drive up from the valley.  Nice night.  Window was open, occasional tail fuzz in the face, it happens.  At the side of the road way ahead, further around the bend, some of those pink forest babies, and some juveniles, the adults stooping and foraging for sandwiches.  The babies round and hairless and ugly, not very mobile, the adults would have to pick them up and carry them from place to place.  Little short squat young ones were waddling about, and as is inevitable with those creatures, an inexperienced little one wandered out into the road just as squirrel was coming up the hill and making the curve.  WHAM!  Crap! he yelled, not having swerved in time.  Damn NUISANCE, stupid little humans, good lord, why can’t those messy things be eradicated.  No matter, no harm done.  Small they were and didn’t cause any damage to speeding vehicles which struck them willy nilly, especially in these rural parts.  Highways too, though.  I mean they were just everywhere.  Seemed to breed indiscriminately any time of year.  Squirrel shook his head, tsk.  Too bad.  Poor stupid fucker, but they should really stay underground where they’re out of our way.

Pink baby smear on the side of the road.  Gooey flesh and guts exploded out like a popped balloon.  The others started at the impact, the male looked up and froze momentarily, sandwich dangling from its mouth, then it moved back slowly toward the woods, making a kind of hooting noise, their alarm sound.  The female took a step toward the road then suddenly ran off, little ones bumbled about awkwardly after their parents.

Soon after the road was quiet again.

 

from memories of a counter girl

ancient guy comes in, deafened, oblivious, robotic… going through the motions of some long-learned ritual, off to the drycleaners, something to do.

he probably lives at the assisted living center nearby.  it’s just so depressing, his being so old and so barely there, in a perpetual daze, but driven to perform this routine as if it’s something he’s been obligated to continue, as though it’s an essential requirement of his remaining humanity.  i imagine with terror myself at an impossible age, spaced out, mumbling incoherently, at the checkout counter with cans of cat food, though all the cats themselves are long gone, addicted to the act of going out for cat food at odd hours… and my apartment is stocked with hundreds of cans of a variety of brands and flavors because even the fading memory of my passed-on companions is finicky.
